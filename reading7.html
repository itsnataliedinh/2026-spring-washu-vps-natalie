Response 7: Do Artifacts Have Ethics?

April 14, 2026

Every time a new technology emerges, we discuss the moral and ethical implications of it. In the case of AI, the use of AI generated images has been normalized, but there's still ethical pushback against it for infringement on artist copyright and overall high water consumption used to power data centers. More companies are adopting AI, selling us the idea that AI is normal as the technology becomes accessible to everyday consumers, yet there's no political enforcement of how images are sourced and produced. There are no laws protecting the likeness of one's image from being re-fabricated and mass-produced.

Like Langdon Winner, I believe technological artifacts are political. The largest global social media, including Meta (Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp), YouTube, and Snapchat are all headquartered in the U.S. While algorithms specifically catering content to each country and individual, the U.S. presence is centered. Social media becomes a tool of hegemony, easily facilitating American politics and cultural ideals. While Facebook has been criticized for not fact-checking political ads and Twitter/X can feel like Elon Musk's personal playground, these apps are no longer just tools for political dissemination. Big tech is inherently political because of the power they leverage over our access to information and personal autonomy. In 2021, Amazon and Google signed a $1.2 billion contract to provide the Israeli state and military with cloud computing and AI systems. The Big Tech x Military Industrial complex lore runs deep.

Big tech doesn't have ethical considerations, often violating our privacy, selling our data, pushing a stream of ads, and creating addictive experiences to keep us scrolling. However, our creation of new technology should be rooted in ethics. The question that stood out most to me is #16: How does this technology empower me? At whose expense? The phrase "whose expense" is impactful because it highlights the constant power dynamic embedded into every action, object, or place that's not always seen from the surface. We all use AI today, whether intentionally typing into ChatGPT or unintentionally listening to music on Spotify, as every app is probbaly using AI-powered algorithms. But has every person in a city consented to their public water or land being used for a data center?

This brings me to question #34: Upon what systems, technical or human, does my use of this technology depend? Are these systems just? As a tech cynic, this reminds me of when Amazon introduced their entirely "cashierless, self-checkout" stores in the U.K., which weren't operated by AI but by humans watching in India... America loves to outsource its labor, probably because foreign labor is easier to exploit. How much of our daily convenience is facilitated by exploiting the Global South?
