Conversations with

Oscar K.

Navigating early playwright career & taking creative risks

Oscar K. is an internationally produced playwright, performance poet, filmmaker, and journalist from Los Angeles. He is based in NYC and LA.

Oscar’s work has been produced in London, NYC, Boston, Cambridge, and Los Angeles, including at The Divine, The Brick, HERE Arts Center, Green Room 42, The Theater Offensive, Boston Center for the Arts, Skylight Theatre, Celebration Theatre, and Harvard College. He has been commissioned to perform poetry for the Los Angeles LGBT Center, the Harvard Museums of Science & Culture, The Huntington, and others. He is a member of the Dramatists Guild of America.

Oscar holds a BA in Women, Gender, and Sexuality Studies and Theater, Dance & Media from Harvard College

Instagram: @velveteen_fxggot

Creative Practice

So I want to begin with your earliest venture into writing. Do you remember the first play that you ever wrote, and can you tell me about it?

O: I guess it started like creative writing. It sounds very pretentious to say, But literally, like as soon as I could read in like first grade or whatever, and I think and was, like initially doing performance poetry, like in Los Angeles. And then, interestingly, my high school had a playwrights festival that was like students could apply and be picked to write a one-act play, which is like a half-hour-long play, and have it staged and produced by a professional director with actors from the school, which is a very unique opportunity. And so I saw the festival and was like, that's cool and fun. And then applied and did the festival for 10th grade. And I guess that was like the first time seeing something I wrote on stage and seeing how a director worked with actors and stuff. So I guess that was like the first play I wrote.

Can you tell me a bit about your creative process when drafting a new play, and where you find inspiration from?

O: So I think the like, the artistic inspiration kind of starts with realizing that an idea or feeling or theme is good enough to commit to. And I think that there is sort of a moment where you're like, “Oh, there is like something under this!” And then, and then sort of being like, “I'm going to follow it.” I think that a lot of the stuff that I wrote, like Narcissus, which is a play that I wrote that is based in New York City, was very much inspired by just meeting very insane people and just having adventures, and I think sort of experiencing the world.

I think specifically for playwriting, because it's, it's just dialogue, like, what people see is just dialog and staging. There's no like interior [narration]. Well, it's not like a novel, with overwriting. I think I have a deadline or a table read, like, people are gonna read it and listen to it. Just imagining what would be the most interesting, sort of crazy thing for people to listen to has actually been really helpful. Unlike other mediums, I think that remembering that there's an audience on the other side of what I'm writing, and I’ve been lucky enough to have audiences that will absorb [my writing] and do what they will with it. So that's kind of the motivation.

How do you deal with creative block, if you have ever stumbled across one?

O: I feel like I'm experiencing that now… so how do I deal with creative block? Well, I used to [write] without structure, put off writing or not have the motivation to unless there's a deadline. I read this actually very helpful Tweet once, which stated that if you have writer's block, or you're trying to create something, and it's not happening, that probably means that you're afraid of something. It’s like a more internal thing. I kind of agree with this, where sometimes it can be easy to sort of say, “Oh, I'm blocked.” It can feel immovable, and you're not gonna do anything about it. You're kind of helpless.

But instead, I think about it as a thing that you can deal with. There's something under it. I guess, there's a deeper reason. On a more practical level, dealing with creative block, or lack of inspiration, or lack of urgency, is to consume other art. Just be in spaces like contemporary art museums, where there’s an environment, or try watching something new. To remind yourself that there's other art out there. And get out of your brain and your thoughts.

So besides playwriting, you mentioned that you also work as a producer, so working with others directors, actors, and even set designers. Was there ever a moment when you were surprised by a fellow collaborator?

O: Wow, there's so many things to choose from. This play that I'm working on right now is called Twink Death of the Universe. And it was commissioned by Celebration Theater, which is a theater in Los Angeles, but basically it's a one-act that I'm trying to expand. And it follows a Korean American gay, trans guy who is obsessed with Korean skincare. This is a good example of that thing where I mentioned setting up rules and clarity around what is and isn't you. Because I know nothing about creating skincare, I know nothing. I don't have a skincare routine. [The character] is trying to run his twink death, and then has to face the trauma he has. The idea of twink death is sort of like ego death.

And I think that I was surprised throughout the process generally, because we were in a writer's room, and so we wrote the plays out. It was me and two other writers who wrote our own separate plays, but then we presented them as a trio of plays under this title of “Trans Lineage”. We wrote the scripts and had support for this for a couple months, and then had rehearsals in Los Angeles. And I think I was surprised by how much fun it can be to work with my director and actors. I was sort of a dramaturg assistant, in a director kind of capacity. I would ask, “Do we want to try this?” And like things to pull out to try, which my director is very generous to take [the suggestions]. And I think that realizing that nothing is really set in stone, and that it can actually be like more fun. It's possible to be serious in some professional sense of making sure things happen, but also to be playful. It's truly like, just enjoying the process with the people that you're with and that amount of time, and then whatever comes out of it. It's like the audience's perception is not the most important thing. I think that was perhaps what I learned that would surprise me.

Subject & Themes

So much of your work humorously blends themes of Korean American identity and also queerness, which can be personal. I wonder how much of your own experience affects the final work that you produce.

O: I think that with all artists, not just playwrights, you're not necessarily able to give a disclaimer or explicitly say, “this scene is something I’ve experienced.” Sometimes it's literally a line of dialog that stuck with me because my friend said it versus this is completely made up. Or actually, [this experience] is not real, but thematically, it's like real.

It all comes from stuff I've experienced or can imagine. I would attend a workshop, and then people assume it's all real. [They see] a Korean, American, gay, trans guy, for example, and it’s sort of easier to see that person as a stand-in for the writer rather than as a fictional character.

As opposed to if I'm writing a character who doesn't have a similar identity, people naturally kind of assume, “Oh yeah, you're probably writing from a more distanced place. It's probably not autobiographical,” even if the character's identities are just different from your own.

Having this concept of deciding how much of your personal self you put into the work and deliberately being like, “This is the part of the story that's not like me, or this character is kind of like me, but except in this very major way.” Sort of creating these boundaries. Being very clear only to yourself. Realize this is not a journal entry, where it’s pure thought. It's being shaped and strategically crafted to do this particular thing.

So humor has a role in your work. You list hyper-niche details in your work; in Narcissus, you shout out the Buffalo Exchange thrift stores in Bushwick. In Twink Death, you use Korean skincare as a device for self-indulgence. You also compare transformation to mortality, relying on dark humor. Previously, you've described yourself as being chronically online. How does internet culture shape your writing style, if at all?

O: I have to teach people how to interact with the thing that you're doing. And you create a rule, and then you break it, and [the audience is] like, “Wow, it's crazy.” I think someone once told me, "Oh, your dialog has the same cadence as your little anecdotes, your Instagram stories." And I was like, “That is a terrible thing to say to me. Stop that.” This is a playwright I really admired. On the Internet, you can reference memes and stupid things that are kind of part of like our general consciousness, which is kind of cool. You could meet someone in a different country, or with a different age, and you don't know much about each other, but then you mention some specific, reference, or post, and they're like, “Oh, yeah!” It's cool, because we both absorb that and have attached meaning to it.

I think there’s intimacy between the person who creates the thing and the people who consume the thing.

I think that is very weird and blurred on the internet. Like, for example, people will be on my story, and I'm telling you about the existential problem I'm having. You know, I'm showing you my food that I'm eating. And this place where I am. But I think that's interesting. People of a certain generation, or like social media usage do this, I think that, as a writer— I don't know if it gives me permission, but like it makes me realize that maybe there are interesting things that like I can write about, that maybe on stage, you wouldn't think would be interesting, or people wouldn't care about but like on your stories. It's really just sharing different things, connecting with people who want connection in different ways.

That line about your humor being the same cadence as your Instagram Stories is an iconic line, and I'm also a fan of your Instagram story. I love to like every little post that I see.

O: I do appreciate my loyal story likers. Like, yes, I would die for all of you.

You know, everyone can be a story liker, but not everyone can be a story spammer. So thank you.

Career

So you recently moved to New York from LA. Can you talk about the experience and how they're different?

O: Yeah, I think it's kind of funny that, like, LA and New York kind of have this rivalry. It kinds of makes me think about work-life balance and where people are putting their energy, and the ways in which they're performing or genuinely engaging in work. For example, I think that the energy of New York, is soon as you step off into the subway from JFK, you're like, “Oh my God.” People are always saying, “No one knows how to walk.” Because people are going places fast, and I enjoy that energy.

And I think in LA also, mostly because people are not really on foot, you don't have as much friction with people, you're kind of in your bubble or your car. I do think in LA, it does feel more laid back, or at least like people are working very hard, of course, but it's not like that people necessarily externally show that.

Probably, in both cities and all cities, people should just not continually be productive and be putting out stuff in a capitalistic world. No one comes to New York City for a chill, balanced life. I don't know if that may be true, and if it may be true for LA as well. But I feel like there should be a way that it can happen.

So I have one final question. You graduated from college nearly one year ago. Do you have any advice for young artists who are just beginning their creative pursuits, and how to navigate a non-linear career?

O:I’m perpetually applying to gigs, mostly part-time, so I have time to create.

Hmm…definitely have a portfolio. The website design itself doesn't have to be that fancy, but it should just have all the relevant information so people can tell other people about you. Make it easy for other people to find you. You should be online all the time. Mostly, I know a lot of people just from DMing them on Instagram and then meeting in person. I like having some sort of online presence where you're able to interact with people that maybe don't live in your city or who are not even in your industry. You might not meet them just out on the street. Some people are like, “I don't like to market my work; it makes me feel bad.” And I would say don't force yourself to do something that'll make [you feel] uncomfortable. But I do think that I've connected with a lot of people this way.

Find what it is that helps you best produce work, if that's something you struggle with just naturally. Sometimes, I need a writing workshop where someone will be waiting for me to give them my writing. I create containers of time.

I think I've been very lucky to have many different mentors, from applying to programs and being connected to people and sort of just asking. Finding mentors is about putting yourself in places where you are able to meet people who have maybe been doing it for longer or who can offer some perspective.

It's a constant thing I'm also thinking about, being around people who are doing this longer than me. Perhaps they have more wisdom and perspective versus people who are your peers. As an artist, it's really important and fun to be like, “yeah, we're all kind of doing it together.” I guess in other disciplines, it’s very easy to be like, “I'm just like doing it by myself on my own project.” We don’t realize how many things require collaboration.

Oh! and I think that having hobbies outside of art is a good interest. It could be like a TV show or something. It could be a specific Wikipedia rabbit hole, so your brain is like chewing on something that is not art-related or is not output-related. You should put yourself in new situations.
